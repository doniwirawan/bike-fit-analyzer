How it works

Three steps, no account, no upload

Everything runs locally in your browser using Google's MediaPipe Pose model. There's no server compute — the video is analyzed on your own hardware and never sent anywhere.

Step 1

Film your ride

Camera square-on at hip height, pedaling steadily for 20–30 seconds. A turbo trainer is ideal. Upload a clip or record with your webcam on the spot.

Step 2

AI tracks your joints

The pose model finds your shoulder, elbow, wrist, hip, knee and ankle in every frame, locks onto your near side, and skips joints it can't clearly see.

Step 3

Get graded advice

Your angles at the bottom of the pedal stroke are graded against research ranges, with a plain-English fix for anything out of range — one change at a time.

What good looks like

What an ideal fit looks like

A good fit balances power, comfort, and control. Here's the target for each key joint — and what it means when you're off.

Knee at the bottom

30–40°

Leg nearly — but not fully — extended at the bottom of the stroke. Too bent means the saddle's too low (knee strain, less power); nearly locked out means it's too high (rocking hips, sore Achilles).

Back / torso angle

40–50°

Low enough to be efficient and aero, but high enough to breathe and stay comfortable for the length of your ride.

Elbow bend

15–30°

Arms softly bent, never locked — they soak up road buzz, help you steer, and take load off your hands.

Reach to the bars

80–95°

A reach that keeps your shoulders relaxed and open — not so long you're stretched thin, not so short you feel cramped.

Stable hips

from the rear

A pelvis that stays level and doesn't rock side to side. Rocking usually means the saddle is a touch too high.

Knee tracking

from the front

Knees driving straight up and down over the pedals, not diving in or bowing out — a cleat and stability cue.

The measurements

Side view — the core fit read

Angles are sampled at the bottom of every pedal stroke and reported as the median across the whole clip, so a single blurry frame can't skew the result. Each is graded green / amber / red against ranges from the Holmes method and dynamic-fit research.

Side view Primary

The reliable read. Saddle height (knee angle) is the number to trust most; reach-related angles are softer on a 2D view.

Technology

The tech under the hood — and why the numbers hold up

No black box. Here's exactly what runs, and the design choices that make the reliable readings trustworthy.

MediaPipe BlazePose

Google's on-device pose model finds 33 body landmarks per frame. It's browser-native and Apache-2.0 licensed — no server, no install.

WebGPU, with WASM fallback

Runs on your GPU where supported for speed, and automatically drops to CPU/WASM elsewhere so it still works.

Deterministic frame stepping

Instead of playing the video, it seeks frame-by-frame so every frame is sampled — no undersampling on slow hardware, no stalls in a background tab.

Median across strokes

Angles are measured at the bottom of every pedal stroke, then the median is taken. One blurry frame or a bad stroke can't move the result.

Near-side lock + visibility gating

It locks onto the leg closest to the camera using confidence over the whole clip, and ignores joints the model isn't sure it can see — so an occluded far leg never poisons the numbers.

Research-based ranges

Green/amber/red thresholds come from the Holmes method and dynamic-fit research, not arbitrary guesses.

ⓘ

How accurate is it, really?

The saddle-height (knee) reading is the one to trust — it's stable and well-validated. Torso and elbow are solid; reach/shoulder is the softest, because a flat 2D side view can't perfectly judge depth. It's a proof-of-concept, so it won't match a 3D pro fit — but for spotting an obviously-off saddle or position, it's genuinely useful. Always change one thing at a time and re-film.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

Is my video uploaded anywhere?

No. All detection and calculation happen in your browser on your device. Nothing is uploaded, there's no account, and there's no server compute.

Which bikes does it work with?

Any bike — road, mountain (MTB), gravel, TT/triathlon, hybrid, commuter, or e-bike. It tracks your body, so the bike doesn't matter. Ranges are tuned for a general road/endurance position, so very aggressive or very upright setups read a little differently.

How accurate is it?

Accurate enough to catch obvious issues. The knee/saddle-height reading is the most reliable; reach is the softest. It's a 2D proof-of-concept, not a replacement for a professional 3D fit.

What kind of clip should I film?

20–30 seconds, camera square-on at hip height, pedaling at an easy steady cadence. A turbo trainer and good lighting give the cleanest read.

Does it work on my phone?

Yes, in modern mobile browsers. Devices with WebGPU are faster; older ones fall back to a slower CPU mode but still work.

Is it free and open source?

Completely free and open source. Browse or contribute to the code at github.com/doniwirawan/bike-fit-analyzer.

Can it replace a professional bike fit?

No. It makes basic fitting accessible and helps you spot big problems, but a perfect fit still needs a qualified fitter's hands-on judgement.

⚠

Basic fitting for everyone — not a replacement for a pro fitter

A perfect fit still needs a professional and their hands-on judgement. What this tool does is make basic fitting accessible: a quick, free way to catch the obvious stuff — saddle far too high or low, wildly off angles — before you ever book a session. It's a 2D side-view estimate, not a 3D pro fit, and it's still a work in progress, actively looking for input from qualified bike fitters to sharpen its ranges and advice. If you're a fitter (or know one), feedback is very welcome — open an issue on GitHub. Treat the results as a starting point, change one thing at a time, and re-film. Not medical advice.
